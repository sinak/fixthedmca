Think you own the electronics you buy?

You can own the object, but the software is a different story.

On October 28th, the

decided not to renew an exemption for
carrier unlocking cell phones*

Your right to unlocking a cell phone without carrier permission is now in legal limbo, to be determined by courts.

The potential punishment?

Up to $500,000 fine and 5 years in jail.

Heard of jailbreaking?

Same situation*.

Jailbreaking lets you run whatever software you want on your device, including programs not from an "app store."

The Librarian of Congress refused to add exemptions for jailbreaking tablets or game consoles.

* For the moment, jailbreaking phones is exempted, but that may change too.

Wondering why the Librarian of Congress, a non-elected official, gets to make these decisions?

It's the same reason that visually impaired people need to ask for an exemption every 3 years to be able to use screen reader technology for ebooks ...

And the same reason some security researchers can't publish information that would make you safer online ...

The Culprit:

"The Anti-Circumvention Provision"

Section 1201 of the Digital Millenium Copyright Act

Originally passed by Congress to protect music labels and the movie industry from piracy, Section 1201 is now being used to prevent Americans from making fair use of the things we buy. The law specifies that the Librarian of Congress can make exemptions every three years.

You can read the librarian's 2012 ruling here.

We started a petition asking the White House to make cell phone unlocking permanently legal, and they responded with support.

In their statement they said it's "common sense" that unlocking should be made legal.

But now we want to go one step further

We're asking Congress to fight for consumer rights and fix the DMCA.

By fixing Section 1201, Congress could make the following clearly and permanently legal:

  • Unlocking cell phones, regardless of contract status
  • Jailbreaking phones, tablets, game consoles, and other electronics
  • Screen reading software for the blind
  • Software and hardware modifications and repairs to products that have been legally purchased
