Think you own the electronics you buy?

You can own the object, but the software is a different story.

On October 28th, the

decided to not renew an exemption for
carrier unlocking cell phones*

Your right to unlocking a cell phone without carrier permission is now in legal limbo, to be determined by courts.

The potential punishment?

Up to $500,000 fine and 5 years in jail.

Heard of jailbreaking?

Same situation*.

Jailbreaking lets you run whatever software you want on your device, including programs not from an "app store."

The Librarian of Congress refused to add exemptions for jailbreaking tablets or game consoles.

* For the moment, jailbreaking phones is exempted, but that may change too.

Wondering why the Librarian of Congress, a non-elected official, gets to make these decisions?

It's the same reason that visually impaired people need to ask for an exemption every 3 years to be able to use screen reader technology for ebooks ...

And the same reason you can't legally make backups of DVDs you purchase ...

The Culprit:

"The Anti-Circumvention Provision"

Section 1201 of the Digital Millenium Copyright Act

Orignally passed by Congress to protect music labels and the movie industry from piracy, Section 1201 is now being used to prevent Americans from making fair use of the things we buy. The law specifies that the Librarian of Congress can make exemptions every three years.

You can read the librarian's 2012 ruling here.
